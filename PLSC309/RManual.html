Opening Data

We need to load the foreign library to load the commands that R needs to open outside data files. Then we use read.dta() or read.csv() to open the data file:

library(foreign)
setwd("~/Dropbox/QPA/Data/2012ANES")
anes <- read.dta("2012ANES.dta")

Note that anes is what I have chosen to call my data object. You can call it anything you want. I chose anes because this data is from the American National Election Studies (the ANES)

Note that the setwd() command is the command that RStudio uses to tell R where my working directory (the folder that R should look for to find my data) is. We set our working directory by navigating the lower-right corner of RStudio to the right file, clicking the gear, and clicking “Set As Working Directory.”

Basic Arithmitic

R works as a calculator. Try some basic arithmetic: 

2 + 2  #addition

## [1] 4

2 * 3  # multiplication

## [1] 6

8 / 2 #division

## [1] 4

4 - 2 #subtraction

## [1] 2

Exponents work with the '' symbol

3^2  

## [1] 9

So do square roots

sqrt(16)

## [1] 4

And absolute values

abs(-15)

## [1] 15

Parentheses control the order of operations.

4 + 5 * 2

## [1] 14

(4 + 5) * 2

## [1] 18

A vector of numbers is represented using the c() command, with numbers separated by commas. Spacing does not matter

c(3, 5, 9)

## [1] 3 5 9

The <- symbol is used to assign a variable to a value

a <- 5 #now R knows that 'a' is equal to 5
a #see?

## [1] 5

We can perform all of the mathematical functions on a now as if it is any other number

a + 3

## [1] 8

Everything is case sensitive, so capital X and lower case x are not the same values!

X <- 6 
x <- 7
c(x, X)

## [1] 7 6

We can easily compute the sum of a vector. This also works with vectors that have been assigned to a variable:

sum(x=c(4, 5, 6, 8, 18))

## [1] 41

m <- c(4, 5, 6, 8, 18)
sum(m)

## [1] 41

Sometimes some of our data is missing. For instance, in survey data, some respondents might refuse to answer certain questions, so we have no value for those people. In R, there is a built in value called 'NA' for these situations. Let's create a vector called fakedata, and make the fourth element missing:

fakedata <- c(5, 6, 7, NA, 8)

Missing data can make our life hard because R doesn't know what to do with it.

sum(x=fakedata)

## [1] NA

Adding na.rm=TRUE to the sum() function tells R to give us the sum of the other four values

sum(x=fakedata, na.rm=TRUE) 

## [1] 26

Working with Vectors

Here is a vector of 8 numbers. I have named it x.numeric, but I could name it anything I wanted to name it.

x.numeric <- c(3, 6, 7, 9, 2, 4, 6, 9)

How long is the vector? length() tells us:

length(x.numeric)

## [1] 8

One thing we might want to do with a vector is access a particular element. Let's ask R what the 4th element in our vector is:

x.numeric[4]

## [1] 9

Or, we could use the : symbol to ask R what the first 4 elements are:

x.numeric[1:4]

## [1] 3 6 7 9

But, maybe we want to know what particular nonconsecutive elements of our vector are:

x.numeric[c(1, 3, 6)] #gives us the first, third, and sixth element

## [1] 3 7 4

we get NA if we ask for an element that doesn't exist:

x.numeric[10] 

## [1] NA

We can also ask R to tell us all particular values of a vector that meet a particular criteria. this gives us all the values equal to 6:

x.numeric[x.numeric==6] 

## [1] 6 6

And this tell us all of the values of the vector less than 5:

x.numeric[x.numeric<5]

## [1] 3 2 4

We can also change a particular element of a vector using the arrow:

x.numeric

## [1] 3 6 7 9 2 4 6 9

x.numeric[4] <- 6 
x.numeric

## [1] 3 6 7 6 2 4 6 9

Do you see how the fourth element in x.numeric changed? It was 9, but we changed it to 6.

Descriptive Statistics

We can use a frequency distribution to see the distribution of variables that are not measured at the interval level. table() allows us to make frequency distributions 

table(anes$voted2008)

## 
## No, Didnt Vote     Yes, Voted 
##           1307           4585

If we want to see how two non-interval variables are related, we can make a two-way crosstabulation (also called a contingency table):

table(anes$voted2008,anes$voted2012)

##                 
##                  No, Didnt Vote Yes, Voted
##   No, Didnt Vote            758        440
##   Yes, Voted                341       3954

To find the average, we use mean() Note that na.rm=TRUE also works for this command, and we have to use it in this example since the age variable contains missing values:

mean(x=anes$age, na.rm=TRUE)

## [1] 49.44

median() tells us the median value of a vector. Note that na.rm=TRUE also works for this command.

median(x=anes$age, na.rm=TRUE)

## [1] 51

Remember that the median is just the .5 quantile. To find quantiles, use the quantile command:

quantile(x=anes$age, probs=.5, na.rm=TRUE)

## 50% 
##  51

Note that summary() tells us the the mean and median of a variable, as well:

summary(anes$age)

##    Min. 1st Qu.  Median    Mean 3rd Qu.    Max.    NA's 
##    17.0    35.0    51.0    49.4    62.0    90.0      60

Graphing Data

To make a histogram, use the hist() command:

hist(anes$age)

The other important thing you can change in a histogram is the number of breaks (the number of bars):

hist(anes$age, breaks=5)

There are lots of other commands you can use to change features of your plot. For example, xlab changes the label of the x-axis to something more informative, main changes the main title of the plot, and col changes the color of your plot.

hist(anes$age, xlab="Age of Survey Respondents", main="Distribution of Ages", col="orange")

We might want to know the relationship between two interval-level variables. We can do this with a scatterplot. In my example below, I am telling R to plot age on the x (horizontal) axis and the percent likelihood that they will vote in the 2012 election on the y (vertical) axis:

plot(x=anes$age, y=anes$pctWillVote)

We can also dress this plot up to look prettier. Many of the arguments to the hist() command will work here, and we can also change the type of dot used by changing the pch (which stands for Point CHaracter) number.

plot(x=anes$age, y=anes$pctWillVote, main="Likelihood of Voting, by Age", xlab="Respondent's Age",ylab="Percent Likelihood of Voting", pch=3, col="orange")

We can make a boxplot. The ~ character is used in formulas in R. The dependent variable goes on the left side of the ~ while the independent variable(s) go on the right-hand side. Let's look at respondents' age based on whether they watch Saturday Night Live.

boxplot(anes$age~anes$snl, main="Age, by SNL Viewership", xlab="Watch SNL?")
