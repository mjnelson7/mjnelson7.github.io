PL SC 309: R Commands

We need to load the foreign library to load the commands that R needs to open outside data files. Then we use read.dta() or read.csv() to open the data file:

library(foreign)
setwd("~/Dropbox/309/Data/2012ANES")
anes <- read.dta("2012ANES.dta",convert.factors=FALSE)

**attach(anes)** tells R to learn the names the variables in your dataset. If you do not attach your data, you must type datasetName$variableName every time you reference a variable. If you do not attach() your dataset, you must type anes$ every time you reference a variable.

sum() adds together all of the values of a vector. Note that we have to add na.rm=TRUE if we have missing values in our vector.

sum(x=c(4, 5, 6, 8, 18))

## [1] 41

sum(x=c(5, 6, 7, NA, 8))

## [1] NA

sum(x=c(5, 6, 7, NA, 8), na.rm=TRUE)

## [1] 26

mean() tells us the average of a vector. Note that na.rm=TRUE also works for this command.

mean(x=c(4, 5, 6, 8, 18))

## [1] 8.2

mean(x=c(5, 6, 7, NA, 8))

## [1] NA

mean(x=c(5, 6, 7, NA, 8), na.rm=TRUE)

## [1] 6.5

median() tells us the median value of a vector. Note that na.rm=TRUE also works for this command.

median(x=c(4, 5, 6, 8, 18))

## [1] 6

median(x=c(5, 6, 7, NA, 8))

## [1] NA

median(x=c(5, 6, 7, NA, 8), na.rm=TRUE)

## [1] 6.5

quantile() gives us a specified quantile of a vector. Note that na.rm=TRUE also works for this command. To get the median (the .5 quantile) of our vector:

quantile(x=c(5, 6, 7, NA, 8), probs=.5, na.rm=TRUE)

## 50% 
## 6.5

Linear Regression

lm() fits a linear model. Our alpha hat is called (Intercept) in the R output and the parameter next to our variable name (Dem2008 in this case) is our estimated beta hat. After we estimate a model, the “object” that R creates to store your model estimates contains a lot of extra information. Let's list those extra items. This includes fitted values and residuals: 

electionData <- read.csv("http://mjnelson.org/PLSC309/prezvote0812.csv")
m <- lm(Dem2012 ~ Dem2008, electionData)

abline() plots a straight line. We can use it to plot our estimated regression line. Note that a is the y-intercept and b is the slope:

plot(electionData$Dem2008, 
     electionData$Dem2012, 
     xlab="Democratic Vote Share 2008", 
     ylab="Democratic Vote Share 2012",
     main="Presidential Vote Share",
     pch=19,
     col="blue")
abline(a=-4.752, b=1.048, lwd=3)

Logistic Regression

glm() calculates a regression line. Remember to include family=binomial in the parentheses so R knows to run a logit. summary() and many of the other commands we've learned for linear regression will also work with logistic regression.

m <- glm(voteObama2012~democrat,anes,family=binomial)
summary(m)
